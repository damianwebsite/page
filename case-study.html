The mission

GeneSprout Initiative was founded in 2016 with a simple goal: create an open dialogue about genetic editing (New Breeding Techniques) and plant science. The challenge was clear — complex topics like CRISPR, gene editing, and plant biotechnology are often misunderstood, and public discourse tends to be polarized rather than informed.

Our approach was to translate cutting-edge plant science into accessible, engaging experiences. Through hands-on workshops, interactive exhibitions, and educational materials, we've reached over 340 participants across 8 schools, fostering curiosity and evidence-based understanding.

"I never knew plants could be so interesting! The workshop made me want to study biology."

— Participant, age 14

Our approach

Hands-on workshops

We developed a series of interactive workshops designed for primary and secondary school students. Each session combines a short lecture with hands-on activities — from extracting DNA from strawberries to discussing ethical questions around genetic modification. The workshops are designed to be:

  • Interactive: Students don't just listen — they do. Every workshop includes a practical experiment or activity.
  • Age-appropriate: Content is tailored to different age groups, from simple plant biology for younger students to ethical debates for older groups.
  • Inquiry-based: We encourage questions and critical thinking, rather than just presenting facts.

Educational materials

To extend the impact beyond the workshop, we created a library of open-access educational materials. These include:

  • Lesson plans for teachers
  • Infographics explaining key concepts
  • Videos and animations
  • Discussion guides for classroom debates

Impact & outcomes

Reach & scale

Over 340 participants across 8 schools in 3 years. Workshop requests grew 40% year-over-year through word-of-mouth.

Knowledge retention

Post-workshop surveys showed 78% of students could explain basic genetic concepts correctly, up from 23% before the workshop.

Community building

Created a network of 12 teachers who now run their own science communication sessions using our materials.

Process & methodology

1

Curriculum development

Designed age-appropriate curricula for 3 age groups (8-10, 11-13, 14-16) in collaboration with teachers and plant scientists.

2

Pilot testing

Ran 3 pilot workshops with 45 students to test materials, refine activities, and gather feedback from teachers and students.

3

Resource creation

Developed open-access materials: lesson plans, infographics, videos, and interactive activities. All materials available under Creative Commons license.

4

School outreach

Partnered with 8 schools across the region, offering free workshops and teacher training sessions to build local capacity.

5

Impact evaluation

Pre- and post-workshop surveys to measure knowledge gain, attitude shifts, and engagement. Continuous iteration based on feedback.

Key learnings

Hands-on works best

Students retain information far better when they can see, touch, and experiment. The DNA extraction activity — where students extract real DNA from strawberries using simple household ingredients — consistently generates the most excitement and curiosity. It's a tangible, memorable experience that makes abstract concepts concrete.

Teachers are multipliers

Training teachers to run their own science communication sessions was one of our most effective strategies. Once a teacher feels confident with the material, they can reach hundreds of students over multiple years. Our network of 12 trained teachers now reaches over 500 students annually.

Complex topics need simple entry points

Genetic editing is a complex topic, but it can be introduced through simple metaphors and analogies. We use the metaphor of "nature's toolkit" — explaining that genes are like tools in a toolbox, and scientists are learning how to use them more precisely.

Future plans

Looking ahead, we're planning to expand the GeneSprout Initiative in several directions:

  • Online presence: Developing a website and YouTube channel to reach a wider audience
  • Teacher training program: Scaling up our teacher training to reach more schools
  • Exhibition: Creating a traveling exhibition about plant science for museums and science centers
  • International expansion: Adapting our materials for different educational contexts and languages

Reflections

The most important lesson from this initiative: science communication is about building bridges, not just transmitting information. It's about creating space for dialogue, for questions, and for genuine curiosity. When people feel heard and respected, they become far more receptive to new ideas — even complex ones like genetic editing.

Looking back, I wish we'd invested more in evaluation from the start. While we have strong anecdotal evidence of impact, more rigorous data collection would have helped us make a stronger case for scaling up the program.
